AUTHOR BIO:

My name is Michael. I’m an echocardiographer currently living in Austin who loves researching new things and leading a data-driven life. I love to spend time with my wonderful family and also enjoy some extracurriculars such as running, cycling, calisthenics and more. My background, deeply rooted in science, causes me to approach new ideas and information with skepticism and adapt with it as it evolves.

Working in cardiology, I'm exposed to various types of people and diseases including the impact stress can have on the cardiovascular system over time. Ever heard of "Heart-break Disease?" The real name is Takotsubo and it's brought on almost entirely by excessive stress. A rush of hormones literally prohibits the left ventricle from pumping efficiently and it requires a bunch of expensive tests and procedures to diagnose. Some other symptoms accompanying stress include palpitations, hypertension, inflammation and in the worst cases, a heart attack or stroke. So if stress can have such a devestating effect on the body, wouldn't the opposite also be true?

My career, love of learning and somewhat traumatic childhood inspired me to write Dare to be Happy. Although life is great now, getting to this point was far from easy and many hard lessons were learned along the way. Let's just say, I'm no stranger to physical and emotional abuse. Eventually, at the age of 16, I ran away from home. This taught me how to couch surf, move on a whim and even collect furniture from dumpsters. There were multiple times I found myself in between places so I was also forced to learn how to survive out of a car. Pro tip: with a gym membership comes showers, toilets and a place to brush your teeth.

Surprisingly, even with all of this instability and lack of direction, I still found a way to stroke my own ego. Was this a learned behavior?

These traumas led to many self-deprecating behaviors over the years that I’m certainly not proud of. Although difficult to admit, the reality is that I was left unhappy all the way through my early 30s and yes, I was married before I turned 30. Savanah and I had our fair share of problems. What's ironic is that I was also able to leverage these undesirable behaviors in a way that also benefited me. The inflated ego and toxic competitiveness actually led me to excel through college and with every job, my responsibilities would increase and my pay would follow. Everything looked good on the surface but it had come at a price. Despite the academic and employment successes, I had nobody in my life outside of Savanah. It was as isolating as my childhood. Sure, temporary relationships would form here and there, but they were gone just as fast. I would cut anybody out over any kind of trivial disagreement regardless of who they were. Fortunately for me, Savanah gave me more chances than most but I was very close to losing her as well. I'm eternally grateful for her commitments and certainly didn't deserve it at the time.

As a data-driven person, what I needed was to pair my situation and behaviors to an explanation. It was the only way I could possibly break free so I decided to connect with a therapist.

In 2022, when I almost lost Savanah, I did just that. It was at this point that I was able to make connections between my self-deprecating behaviors and my childhood. There was no trust, empathy or emotional intelligence but rather selfishness, ego and poor communication skills. I had to figure out how to develop these things as an adult but finally, I had control over my feelings and eventually my life.

So imagine, what kind of impact do you think these epiphanies would have on someone who loves to learn and lead a data-driven life? You guessed it, more research and more learning! Perhaps this is yet another example of a learned behavior stemming from my childhood but I digress. The next step was to learn everything I could about happiness. Every time I discovered a new concept, I implemented it. Then, after some continued practice, tweaking and reflection, I had a framework that could be utilized by anyone interested in improving their own personal well-being. It was simply too valuable to keep to myself!

The entire process of writing this book was literally fueled by my own personal experiences and reflections. While putting it together, I continued to practice and continued to improve upon my own happiness and personal relationships. In fact, today my marriage is better than day one. I even managed to rekindle relationships with old friends and family members I had previously deemed as unsalvageable.

So here I am, hoping to help you conquer your own personal struggles. I’ve blended science with experience and formulated a method that puts the control back in your hands. By the end of this book, if you end up taking it seriously, I guarantee that your mindset will be different. Your relationships will fortify, your body will feel better and you’ll learn how to find peace through patience, empathy and self-compassion. I’ll dive into the science behind the “why” then help you figure out the “how.”
